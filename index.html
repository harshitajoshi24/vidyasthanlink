How it works

From empty timetable slot to a live, taught class.

01

College lists the gap

A college marks a subject or slot where they don't have strong in-house faculty — say, a specific elective or a subject only one weak professor currently covers.

02

An educator is matched

Vidyalink matches the slot to a vetted subject educator — assessed on teaching clarity, not just credentials — who can commit to that recurring class time.

03

Class runs live, to many colleges

The educator teaches once, live, streamed into classrooms at several colleges simultaneously — with local staff present for discipline, doubts, and attendance.

Built for both sides

A better deal for colleges. A bigger reach for educators.

FOR COLLEGES

Fill teaching gaps without a full hire

  • Cover low-enrolment or specialist subjects without a dedicated faculty seat.
  • Pay per class or per semester slot, not a full salary.
  • Local staff still handle attendance, discipline, and in-person doubt sessions.
FOR EDUCATORS

Teach once, reach many classrooms

  • One prepared lecture is delivered live to several colleges — same effort, wider income.
  • Selected on teaching clarity, with student ratings visible upfront.
  • Consistent recurring slots instead of one-off gig work.
Before you ask

The honest questions colleges bring up first.

Not on its own, in most cases — many universities require a UGC-recognised faculty member of record for credit-bearing courses. Vidyalink is built to sit alongside that requirement: the in-house faculty member of record stays responsible for grading and accreditation, while the visiting educator co-teaches the live content. This is a model to validate with each university's rules, not a workaround for them.
Sessions are recorded automatically, so a dropped connection means a short delay, not a lost class — local staff can resume from the recording and the live feed reconnects when the connection is back. Colleges without reliable bandwidth would need a local fallback plan before piloting this.
Educators would go through a sample-taught lesson reviewed by both students and staff before being matched to a live slot, and ongoing student ratings stay visible to colleges — the same accountability a good YouTube educator already earns, just applied deliberately before day one.
Local college staff stay present in the room for every session — attendance, classroom discipline, and in-person follow-up doubts remain their responsibility. The visiting educator owns the teaching; the college keeps ownership of the students.
